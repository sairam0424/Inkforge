A few weeks ago I wrote about how feature flags fail at scale.

Knight Capital. $440M. 45 minutes. A flag key that was never properly archived, reused eight years later, and quietly activated dead trading code on one of nine servers.

I wasn't writing about that incident as a cautionary tale.

I was writing about it because I had lived a version of it.

---

2am. Dashboard red. My lead on the call, calm as always, asks the one question I couldn't answer:

"Which flags are on right now?"

200+ flags across 12 services. No consolidated answer. We dug through Slack threads, opened Confluence, made educated guesses. We eventually found the root cause — a flag key deleted and recreated six months later by a different team, the old Redis-cached targeting rules never invalidated.

47 minutes of investigation. It should have been a 5-minute rollback.

---

That incident is why I wrote about feature flags at scale.

And it is why I spent the next several months building Tombstone.

Tombstone is the system I wish had existed that night.

Three things it does that I couldn't find elsewhere:

Flag keys are permanently archived when retired. They can never be reused. The Knight Capital failure mode, closed by design.

When production breaks, it automatically correlates the last 30 minutes of flag changes against the incident timeline and surfaces the top 3 suspects — ranked, with one-click rollback links. Suspects ready before you've finished reading the alert.

If a flag causes error rate to cross 5%, it rolls back automatically. You wake up to a report, not an escalation.

Everything runs locally. One command. MIT licensed.
